[Collaboration is a] process in which autonomous or semi-autonomous actors interact through formal and informal negotiation, jointly creating rules and structures to govern their relationships and ways to act or decide on the issues that brought them together; it is a process of shared norms and mutually beneficial interactions.

The Context and Challenge of Collaboration

  • Diverse Boundaries: Both anthropogenic and natural distinctions impact collaboration.
  • Variable Decision-Making: Differences in rules and processes complicate consensus.
  • Inconsistent Accountability: Mechanisms vary across different frameworks.
  • Temporal Disparities: Collaboration occurs across varying time scales.
  • Diverse Needs and Demands: Stakeholders have varied priorities and resources.
  • Institutional Fragmentation: Multiple, separate entities with partial overlaps in authority.
  • Overlapping Authorities: Incomplete but overlapping jurisdiction complicates governance.
  • Collective Action Dilemmas: Challenges in organizing group efforts towards common goals.
  • Complexity and Uncertainty: Difficulties in predicting outcomes and making informed decisions.
  • Policy Evolution: Ongoing changes in first and second-generation policy challenges.
  • Climate Change Impact: Adds an additional layer of complexity and urgency to collaborative efforts.

Enter Watershed Management

  • Initiation in the mid-1990s, driven by EPA enforcement of Total Maximum Daily Load (TMDL) provisions of the Clean Water Act §303(d) amid legal pressures.
  • Necessity for a broad range of partners to represent both environmental and economic interests and to tackle collective action problems.
  • Employment of flexible and adaptive policy tools that are informed by ongoing scientific research.
  • Acknowledgment of local conditions both terrestrial and aquatic, complementing traditional regulatory frameworks.
  • Diversity in the organization of collaborative efforts—there is no single correct approach.

Our Model

Research Design in Three Phases

  1. Multilevel Modeling: Implement a multilevel mixed-effects model to analyze characteristics at both the state and watershed levels.
  2. Binary Analysis: Develop a binary logistic model to examine the influence of partnerships and stakeholder perceptions on outcomes.
  3. Mixed Methods Analysis: Utilize a mixed methods approach to explore network characteristics and stakeholder activities, providing a comprehensive understanding of interactions.

Early Findings

  • Impact of Regional NGOs: Introducing each additional NGO is linked to a 4% improvement in water quality.
  • Demographic Influence: Areas with a higher percentage of white populations typically experience better water quality.
  • Historical Data Comparison: Water quality in 2002 compared to 2010 shows improvement (41% insufficient in 2010 vs. 49% insufficient in 2002), indicating a moderate correlation over time (Correlation coefficient, ρ^ = .39; Significance, p < 0.01).
  • State-Level Influence: Around 30% of the variability in water quality can be explained by state-level factors.

Early Findings (cont.)

  • Demographic Impact: Higher proportions of white populations are correlated with an increased perception of water quality improvement.
  • Urban Density: More densely populated areas tend to report better perceived water quality improvements.
  • Effect of Partnerships: Watershed partnerships positively impact public perception of water quality improvements.
  • Statistical Evidence of Partnership Impact: The odds of perceiving improvements in water quality are 0.93 (Confidence Interval: 0.85 to 1.00) when a watershed partnership is involved.

Going Forward

  1. Model Analysis: Conduct a deeper analysis of the hypothetical model to better understand and establish causal relationships among variables.
  2. Network Analysis: Employ network analysis techniques to examine and compare the dynamics among actors involved in water quality management.
  3. Operational Scope: Investigate initiatives and actions beyond basic operational activities to assess their impact on water quality.

The Big Question:

Does Collaboration Matter?

  • While definitive answers remain elusive, our early findings provide intriguing insights.
  • This study is unique in its large-n data set, specifically examining water quality and collaboration across American watersheds.

Research Design in Three Phases

Phase 1: A Multilevel Mixed-Effects Modeling Approach

$$ \begin{aligned} \text{Watershed Level:}\quad & Y_{ij} = \beta_{0j} + \beta_{1j}X_{ij} + r_{ij} \\ \text{State Level:}\quad & \beta_{0j} = \gamma_{00} + \gamma_{01}W_j + u_{0j} \\ & \beta_{1j} = \gamma_{10} + \gamma_{11}W_j + u_{1j} \end{aligned} $$

Phase 2: A Binary Model (cont.)

$$\frac{\text{Pr}(y=1|\mathbf{x})}{\text{Pr}(y=0|\mathbf{x})} = \frac{\text{Pr}(y=1|\mathbf{x})}{1-\text{Pr}(y=1|\mathbf{x})},$$

where $y=$ aggregate organizational responses about whether water quality is perceived improved (1) or not (0) since involvement in the watershed.

Phase 3: A Mixed Methods Approach

  • Organizations asked to name most recent activity conducted related to water quality improvement.
  • Using pattern matching to tease out themes and trends, responses were separated by partnership membership and organizational type and then coded by operational-level activities.
